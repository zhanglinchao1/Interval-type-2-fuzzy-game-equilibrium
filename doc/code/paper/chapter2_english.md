# Chapter 2 Related Work

This chapter reviews studies related to interval type-2 fuzzy game equilibrium for blockchain-governed collaborative communication among physical agents. Existing work mainly involves three directions: blockchain governance and game-theoretic collaborative scheduling, fuzzy state modeling and resource feasibility evaluation, and equilibrium solving and evolutionary stability analysis under fuzzy payoffs.

## 2.1 Blockchain Governance and Game-Theoretic Collaborative Scheduling

Physical agents usually have capabilities for environmental perception, communication interaction, edge computing, strategic decision-making, and physical action. Research on embodied multi-agent systems discusses multi-agent collaboration mechanisms from open-loop, closed-loop, and dual-loop control structures, providing a conceptual basis for modeling vehicles, RSUs, and edge servers as physical agents [1].

Trusted interaction is a prerequisite for resource sharing and collaborative decision-making in collaborative communication among physical agents. With decentralization, tamper resistance, traceability, and smart contract execution, blockchain has been applied to identity authentication, behavior recording, reputation updating, and resource transaction constraints in the Internet of Vehicles. Razafimanjato et al. systematically reviewed blockchain-based IoV trust management, and pointed out that blockchain can provide a transparent, tamper-resistant, and decentralized trust management foundation for complex vehicular environments [2]. Zhao et al. proposed a blockchain-based trust management model for VANETs, incorporating vehicle behavior evidence and trust evaluation into an on-chain trusted management framework [3]. These studies provide a foundation for trusted evidence anchoring and reputation-state maintenance. However, they provide limited analysis of how on-chain governance parameters enter strategic payoff structures and affect equilibrium stability.

Game theory provides modeling tools for resource collaboration, task offloading, and strategy selection among multiple physical agents. Sun et al. proposed BARGAIN-MATCH, which models resource allocation and task offloading in vehicular edge computing as a combination of a bargaining game and a matching game, and proves its stability, weak Pareto optimality, and polynomial complexity [4]. Jiang et al. modeled distributed IoV service offloading as a potential game, and combined graph attention networks to optimize service offloading decisions in mobile edge computing [5]. These methods can characterize competitive and cooperative relationships among vehicles, RSUs, and edge servers. However, most of them are still based on deterministic utility functions, making it difficult to express payoff intervals and fuzzy dominance relations under the joint effects of trusted interaction, communication timeliness, resource feasibility, and on-chain governance weights.

## 2.2 Fuzzy State Modeling and Resource Feasibility Evaluation

Collaborative communication among physical agents has high dynamics, strong coupling, and multi-source uncertainty. In the Internet of Vehicles, link handover, channel congestion, task-delay constraints, computing-load fluctuations, and changes in behavioral trustworthiness make it difficult to fully characterize trusted interaction, communication timeliness, and resource redundancy with a single deterministic scalar. These states are more suitable for representation as membership degrees with fuzzy boundaries.

Fuzzy logic has been used to handle uncertainty in task offloading and resource scheduling in the Internet of Vehicles. Trabelsi et al. proposed a fuzzy-based task offloading method for IoV edge computing, using fuzzy logic to perform dynamic priority judgment and node selection according to task urgency and edge-node resource status [6]. Ehtisham et al. proposed a fuzzy-logic-based IoV/VANET task scheduling method to reduce task offloading latency and improve response performance [7]. These studies show that fuzzy logic can support context-aware state evaluation and resource selection, but their outputs are mainly membership degrees or aggregated scores.

Higher-order fuzzy models further express uncertainty in the membership degree itself. Castillo et al. reviewed Type-3 fuzzy logic systems and their control applications, and pointed out that higher-order fuzzy systems can be used for uncertainty representation in complex control and intelligent computing scenarios [8]. For resource feasibility evaluation, previous work proposed a prediction-enhanced two-stage T-norm-Choquet-OWA resource aggregator, which maps CPU, bandwidth, energy, and power-consumption states into the resource feasibility membership degree $\mu_{\mathrm{res}}$ and proves its monotonicity, boundary correctness, bottleneck priority, and Lyapunov stability [9]. These studies provide a modeling basis for state inputs such as $\mu_{\mathrm{trust}}$, $\mu_{\mathrm{delay}}$, and $\mu_{\mathrm{res}}$, but the connection from state membership degrees to interval type-2 fuzzy payoffs and multi-agent equilibrium still requires further formulation.

## 2.3 Fuzzy Payoff Equilibrium Solving and Evolutionary Stability

Collaborative communication among multiple physical agents requires not only a single scheduling result, but also long-term stability of population strategies under repeated interactions. Existing game-theoretic resource scheduling methods usually use best response iteration, potential game dynamics, matching-based iteration, or multi-agent learning to drive system convergence. BARGAIN-MATCH obtains stable resource allocation through a bargaining game and a matching game [4]. Potential-game-based IoV service offloading constructs a potential function so that local strategy improvement remains consistent with global objective optimization [5]. These methods provide a basis for multi-agent strategy convergence under deterministic payoffs.

Under interval type-2 fuzzy payoffs, payoff comparison is itself uncertain. Directly using the interval center may ignore changes in dominance relations caused by payoff upper and lower bounds, while directly comparing intervals may lead to non-orderability or weak ordering. Existing fuzzy game studies have examined equilibrium existence and fuzzy Nash solutions under fuzzy payoffs. Studies on fuzzy Pareto-Nash equilibrium and type-2 fuzzy Nash equilibria indicate that equilibrium concepts under fuzzy payoffs and fuzzy strategies need to be handled through intervalization, scalarization, or hierarchical cut-set mechanisms [10], [11]. However, most of these studies remain at the level of static or finite games, and have not formed a unified analytical framework with on-chain governance and continuous strategy evolution.

Blockchain governance also gives physical-agent collaborative systems a two-time-scale structure. Off-chain strategies can be updated quickly within communication time slots, whereas on-chain reputation states, smart contract parameters, and rule weights are usually adjusted slowly according to block height or governance cycles. Existing blockchain trust management studies can handle on-chain reputation updates, evidence recording, and trusted execution [2], [3], while existing game-theoretic resource scheduling studies can handle off-chain strategy convergence [4], [5]. However, when on-chain rule weights act as slow variables that change the payoff function, whether the original strategy equilibrium remains stable still requires a unified analysis.
